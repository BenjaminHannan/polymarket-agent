# Polyagent: Top Levers for a Sophisticated Polymarket Paper-Trading Bot

## TL;DR
- **The single biggest lever is your execution model, not your predictor.** The 2026 literature on Polymarket (Della Vedova; Akey/Grégoire/Harvie/Martineau; Yang) is unambiguous: execution explains ~99% of P&L variance among human traders, profits accrue to wallets posting passive limit orders, and your VWAP paper fills are systematically optimistic by roughly half-spread (650–900 bps on low-probability deciles). Replacing VWAP simulation with a queue-aware, taker-vs-maker-aware fill simulator — and re-orienting the strategy toward marketable‑to‑passive limit posting — is the highest-impact change you can make and is fully paper-tradeable.
- **The second-biggest lever is swapping the LightGBM objective from pointwise `binary` to listwise `lambdarank`/`rank_xendcg`, with NegRisk/`event_id` as the query group, and adding live microstructure features into training rather than only at runtime.** This is the same finding from your equities work, but it has a structural reason to be even larger here: NegRisk events are *exactly* learning-to-rank queries (mutually exclusive outcomes that must sum to 1), and the literature on cross-sectional LTR in finance (Poh/Lim/Zohren/Roberts 2021) shows ~3× Sharpe uplift versus pointwise-then-sort.
- **The third lever — paper-tradeable, compounds with your existing combiner — is adding a retrieval-augmented LLM forecaster as a heavily-weighted expert in the per-category log-pool.** Halawi et al. (NeurIPS 2024), the ForecastBench leaderboard (Karger et al., ICLR 2025; Oct-2025 update showing GPT-4.5 at Brier 0.101 vs superforecasters 0.081), and Turtel et al. 2025 ("LLMs Can Teach Themselves to Better Predict the Future") show that retrieval-augmented and DPO-self-play fine-tuned LLMs in the 7–14B range deliver Brier scores at or near human crowd performance, exactly the regime your question-only LightGBM cannot reach because it has no world-state.

Everything else on your idea list (LP market making, multi-LLM consensus, wash-trade filter, RL sizing, multi-stage classifier, Chronos-Bolt) is second-order and should not be touched until the three above are in place.

## Key Findings

1. **Polymarket is efficient enough that prediction skill alone loses money.** Della Vedova (SSRN 6191618, Feb 2026; 222M trades, Nov 2022 – Feb 2026) decomposes returns into directional (forecast) and execution (entry-price) components. Retail traders pick the right side 51.3% of the time and lose $79M; bots pick the right side at coin-flip rates and earn $133M. The two skill dimensions are nearly orthogonal (ρ≈0.13). Across all human trader cohorts, "no trader type beats the price-implied accuracy benchmark." Bots pay 2.52¢ less per contract than casual traders, and **that gap alone flips the sign of returns**. Akey et al. (SSRN 6443103, Mar 2026) confirm: top 1% capture 76.5% of gains, and these gains "flow almost entirely to sophisticated traders who trade using limit orders at prices that are advantageous relative to realized outcomes." Yang (Augusta U., Mar 2026) on 150M trades finds skilled top-5% accuracy traders earn $121/market as makers vs $63/market as takers.

2. **Polymarket spreads and queue dynamics make VWAP simulation badly wrong.** The Dubach (arXiv 2604.24366) microstructure study reports 650–900 bps half-spreads on the lowest-probability decile, depth concentrated in a near-uniform geometric grid (so partial fills cascade across price levels), and trade-direction inference from the public WebSocket feed agrees with on-chain ground truth only 59% of the time — meaning your paper-trader's "fill price" inferred from the WSS feed is itself biased. The IMDEA NBA arbitrage study (arXiv 2605.00864) finds intra-market arbitrages persist a median of 3.6 seconds before automated participants close them — your 30s and 60s scanners are an order of magnitude too slow to capture latent inefficiencies and can only capture structural/persistent edges (NegRisk rebalancing, fee-buffered LP-style edges).

3. **NegRisk markets are the natural query group for a learning-to-rank objective.** A NegRisk event is a set of N mutually exclusive binary outcomes whose YES prices must sum to 1. Saguillo et al. (arXiv 2508.03474) document $29M of arbitrage extracted from NegRisk rebalancing alone (73% of all realized arbitrage on Polymarket Apr 2024–Apr 2025), with 90% of NegRisk liquidity concentrated in the top-4 outcomes — the long tail is where mispricings live. A pointwise binary LGBM cannot exploit the constraint that prices within an event must sum to 1; a listwise LambdaRank objective with `group = event_id` directly optimizes the ranking of mispriced outcomes within each event slate.

4. **Cross-sectional learning-to-rank in finance ≈3× Sharpe vs pointwise.** Poh, Lim, Zohren, Roberts (J. Financial Data Science 3(2), 2021; arXiv 2012.07149) show that swapping a regression/classification head for LambdaMART/ListNet on cross-sectional momentum produces "approximately threefold boosting of Sharpe Ratios." Burdorf (SSRN 5255258, 2025) replicates across asset classes. The mechanism — concentrating model capacity on getting the *order* right rather than the *level* right — applies almost verbatim to a portfolio of prediction-market positions where you are choosing which markets to deploy risk capital into.

5. **LLMs have closed most of the gap to human superforecasters and can be run locally on your 5070 Ti.** ForecastBench (Karger et al., ICLR 2025; Oct 2025 update at forecastingresearch.org): superforecasters Brier 0.081, GPT-4.5 0.101, median public 0.107 (the public was #2 a year ago and is now #22). Halawi et al. (NeurIPS 2024) hit near-crowd Brier with retrieval + GPT-4 fine-tune. Turtel/Wood/Khoja/Mehl (arXiv 2502.05253) show DPO self-play fine-tuning delivers 7–10% Brier improvement over the base model on **Phi-4 14B and DeepSeek-R1 14B** — both fit comfortably in 16.3 GB VRAM at 4-bit quant, and their result was *with* 4-bit quant. Karkare et al. (arXiv 2412.18544) propose arbitrage-based consistency checks correlated with future Brier — a free in-flight QA signal.

6. **Calibration is structured by domain and horizon.** Le (arXiv 2602.19520, "Decomposing Crowd Wisdom") finds politics is systematically underconfident (slopes 1.31 on Polymarket, 1.64 on Kalshi), weather and entertainment are overconfident, and the dominant pattern is a domain × horizon interaction explaining 26% of variance. Generic isotonic calibration on backfilled historical markets — your current setup — collapses this structure. Per-(category × horizon-bucket) calibration tables are a low-effort win.

7. **The "smart money" edge is an execution edge, not a forecasting edge.** Solidus Labs (Apr 2026) found 0.55% of profitable maker wallets capture 50% of maker gains; 0.26% of profitable taker wallets capture nearly the same share. The Goldsky/subgraph adverse-selection idea on your list is therefore best framed as **"detect when a known sophisticated maker is on the other side of my proposed trade"** — i.e., it's an execution-quality filter, not a directional signal.

## Details

### LEVER 1 (highest leverage, immediately paper-tradeable): Realistic execution model and a pivot toward maker-side posting

**Why this is high-leverage given your architecture.** Your current paper trader uses VWAP fills; your trade hunter ranks markets by "edge × category-throttle" and dispatches the top 3 every 30 seconds. This implicitly assumes you cross the spread and sweep the book at quoted depth. Three independent 2026 papers (Della Vedova; Akey et al.; Yang) show that **this is the wrong side of the trade**: in zero-fee Polymarket markets the maker–taker spread transfer is the dominant component of P&L for everyone except the very top of the wallet distribution. Della Vedova quantifies it: 2.52¢/contract execution gap accounts for the *sign* of returns. The microstructure literature (Dubach 2604.24366) reports 650–900 bps half-spreads on low-probability contracts; this is roughly the size of an entire month's "edge" in your paper book. **Your current +$75 over 120 fills could be entirely a half-spread illusion.**

**Concrete implementation path.**

1. **Replace VWAP with a queue-aware fill simulator.** The reference architecture is `hftbacktest` (open-source; readthedocs). Its `ProbQueueModel` family (Probability/Square/PowerN queue position models) is specifically designed for CLOB markets with persistent resting liquidity — exactly Polymarket's near-uniform depth-grid (Dubach SF2). Adapt this rather than rewriting from scratch. Key components to model:
   - **Aggressor side**: when your simulated marketable order arrives, deduct depth from the opposite side at each level you sweep through, in priority order.
   - **Queue position for passive orders**: when you post a limit, your fill probability is conditional on (a) the queue ahead of you being depleted by takers, and (b) no adverse mid-price move that leaves your order stale. Cont/Kukanov/Stoikov's queue-imbalance literature (Gould–Bonart 2016, Moallemi 2016) gives closed-form fill-probability surfaces conditional on top-of-book queue and OBI.
   - **Latency model**: your WSS feed has a sub-50 ms median ingestion delay with a multi-second tail (Dubach SF6). Use an empirical latency distribution from your own pipeline plus Polygon block time (~2 s). Refuse to record fills against book states older than your latency p99.
   - **Slippage on cancellation**: model a finite cancel latency (Polygon block) so you cannot dodge adverse selection costlessly.

2. **Treat VWAP as the optimistic bound and add a "pessimistic" parallel simulator.** Run BOTH simulators on every signal. The pessimistic one assumes you cross the full spread, lose queue priority on every replenishment, and pay full half-spread on entry and exit. **Only deploy real money when the strategy is profitable in the pessimistic simulator.** This is the right operationalization of your current "until I see evidence of success" gate.

3. **Pivot the trade hunter from taker-orientation to passive maker-orientation.** Concretely: replace `dispatch_top_3` (which crosses the spread on highest-edge markets) with a **two-sided limit-order poster** that quotes inside the spread at e.g. mid ± (k_min × σ_short) on markets where your model probability is within an envelope of the mid. This converts your stat-LGBM edge from "an information edge that gets eaten by half-spread" into "an information edge plus the half-spread itself." Yang's evidence is that skilled traders earn ~$121 making vs $63 taking. **You need the LP code path you said is "blocked on real money" to be your default code path even in paper trading**, because the right comparison for go/no-go is what the *posted-passive* strategy looks like, not the taker strategy.

4. **Adverse-selection gating via Goldsky subgraph.** When you post a passive limit, you are giving free optionality to anyone with better information. The empirically dominant adverse-selection signal in Polymarket is **"a known sophisticated maker is on the other side"** (Yang 2026; Solidus 2026). Practical pipeline: pull the rolling 30-day top-K profitable makers from a Goldsky/Jbecker subgraph join (Jon-Becker/prediction-market-analysis is a free public dataset; PolyEdge/Bullpen are commercial alternatives), maintain a `known_smart_makers` set in Redis, and when one of them is on the opposite side at your price, *don't post* (or post only deeper inside the spread to compensate for AS).

**Expected magnitude.** Della Vedova's 2.52¢/contract execution gap, on a $10K NAV, with average ticket size ~$50–100 and fills costing roughly half-spread (~3¢ on mid-probability contracts), implies that maker-vs-taker conversion is worth +1.5 to +3% of NAV per round-turn captured — far larger than realistic predictive-edge gains in efficient categories. The deflation effect on your *paper* edge from VWAP→queue-aware simulation will likely be 50–100% (i.e., your current +$75 paper P&L could go negative). That is feature, not bug: it is the literature telling you not to deploy yet.

**Risk and tradeoffs.** Maker-side strategies have non-trivial inventory risk and require working order management, cancel-on-tick logic, and stale-quote protection — you have many of these gates already, but they were designed for taker flow. Maker losses are dominated by adverse-selection events (resolution-source breaks, oracle disputes), so the existing 16-gate risk layer needs to be re-tuned for the new failure mode (large position sitting on the wrong side of a UMA dispute), not the old one (paying too much spread).

**What does NOT work here.** Latency arbitrage in the strict sense is gone — IMDEA reports a median 3.6 s lifetime for NBA intra-market arbs, you are on Windows over public Polygon RPC, you cannot win that race. Don't waste time trying to. Capture structural maker edge instead.

### LEVER 2 (high leverage, paper-tradeable, compounds with everything you have): LambdaRank objective with NegRisk/event_id query groups, plus microstructure features in the training matrix

**Why this is high-leverage given your architecture.** Your LightGBM is `binary` objective on 422 features (38 hand-coded + 384-dim BGE embeddings of the *question text only*). Microstructure (book imbalance, momentum, news velocity, sentiment z-score) is computed at runtime in `combined.py` and fed to the per-category combiner, but **never seen during LightGBM training**. This is exactly the leak you described, and it has two distinct fixes that should be done together:

**Fix 2a — Listwise objective with `group = event_id` (or NegRisk market_id).** A NegRisk event with N mutually exclusive outcomes is, mathematically, an LTR query with N candidates whose true relevance labels are (1, 0, 0, …, 0). LightGBM exposes this directly via `LGBMRanker(objective='lambdarank')` with `group=[len(event_1), len(event_2), …]`. Two reasons this is the right structural choice:

- **Within-event ranking** is the actual decision problem. You don't bet "p(Trump wins) is mispriced in absolute terms"; you bet "of the N outcomes in this election, k is most mispriced relative to its true conditional probability." LambdaRank's NDCG-weighted gradient assigns higher loss to swap errors at the top of the ranking, which is exactly where capital is allocated.
- **Across-event ranking** for capital allocation. Using `group = day` (one query per trading day) and a longer truncation level lets the model rank markets by where you should deploy a given $X. Poh et al. 2021 reports ~3× Sharpe on cross-sectional momentum from this exact mechanic; the analogue here is allocating risk-capital across the 1,150 books you stream.

Practical detail: lambdarank's outputs are *not* calibrated probabilities. Keep your isotonic calibration as a post-hoc step (fit a separate isotonic map per category × horizon-bucket on a held-out fold). This dual-stage approach is standard in LTR-for-finance: lambdarank for ordering, isotonic for absolute level. CatBoost's `YetiRank` is an alternative with built-in noise robustness; `rank_xendcg` is LightGBM's noise-robust variant — both are tier-A choices and worth a 5-fold CPCV bake-off (see Lever 3-adjacent validation note).

**Fix 2b — Add live microstructure as actual training features.** The reason you saw "AUC = 1.0 per category" partial-leakage on the 6h pre-resolution market price is that the market price is dominating after most uncertainty has resolved. The fix is *not* to drop late-game features but to train on **point-in-time snapshots throughout the market's life**, sampled non-uniformly so that early-life snapshots (where edge actually lives) are upweighted. Concretely:

- For each of your 9,933 historical resolved markets, generate K=10–20 snapshots at log-spaced intervals from market open to resolution.
- At each snapshot, compute and store: (i) mid price; (ii) top-of-book queue imbalance; (iii) multi-level OFI (Cont, Kukanov, Stoikov 2014 — the literature consensus is OFI subsumes trade imbalance and is the dominant short-horizon LOB feature, per the Emergent Mind synthesis); (iv) microprice (Stoikov 2018 — provably better short-horizon predictor than mid); (v) realized vol over multiple windows; (vi) news count + sentiment-z over the same windows; (vii) time-to-resolution.
- Each snapshot is a training row with label = the binary outcome (which is constant per market) and `group = market_id` (or `event_id` for NegRisk). The realized P&L of "buy at snapshot price, hold to resolution" is then a function the model is implicitly learning.
- **This expands your training set from 9,933 to ~100K–200K rows without new resolutions**, which materially improves your fit-stability signal (and is the right answer to "120 paper fills is too small" — train and validate on the historical rows, *use* paper fills only for final live-shift detection).

**Validation discipline (the equities-work transfer).** Apply your CPCV/DSR/PBO discipline directly:

- **CPCV with `group = market_id` purging**: when a market resolves at time `t`, every snapshot of that market (across the full lifecycle) must be in the same fold. Otherwise you leak future price into past snapshots. This is the prediction-market analogue of de Prado's "label horizon" purging — markets are your "labels" and the embargo is "until that market is resolved." `skfolio.model_selection.CombinatorialPurgedCV` with a custom group key is the cleanest implementation.
- **Deflated Sharpe** on the resulting backtest using realistic execution (Lever 1) — not the paper VWAP — with `n_trials` set to the number of objective/feature configurations you tested. Bailey/López de Prado (SSRN 2460551) DSR formula penalizes you for the non-normality and the multiple-testing count; given that you've explicitly mentioned testing 6 model variants (lambdarank, YetiRank, iTransformer, CatBoost, TabPFN, Kronos), `n_trials ≥ 6 × hyperparameter combos` is the right number to plug in.
- **Probability of Backtest Overfitting** (Bailey/Borwein/López de Prado): resample CPCV paths and compute the rank stability of the chosen configuration. PBO above 0.5 means the chosen config is no better than median; that's your hard kill criterion before deploying real money.
- **Live distribution shift detector**: keep a rolling 200-fill calibration table on paper fills and compare to the static isotonic map via PSI or KL. Your stated concern that "calibration done only on backfilled markets — live distribution may shift" is exactly what a live ECE monitor catches.

**Expected magnitude.** Combined effect of (lambdarank objective + within-event grouping + microstructure features in training + per-category × horizon calibration) is conservatively a 10–20% relative reduction in calibrated Brier on validation, and — more importantly — a meaningful uplift in *top-of-list precision* (the metric that actually matters when you pick the top-3 markets each scan). The Poh et al. 3× Sharpe figure is from a different domain (equity momentum) and won't transfer directly, but the *direction* is strong, and the structural fit (NegRisk = native query group) is better than equities.

**Risks and tradeoffs.** Lambdarank gives unrescaled scores; you must keep an isotonic head. Snapshot-based training inflates the row count and creates within-market correlation; CPCV with market-level purging is mandatory, not optional, or you'll get optimistic fold scores. The historical microstructure feature build is genuine data engineering work — you need a tick archive (your WSS capture going forward; Jbecker's public 671M-trade dataset for backfill) and parquet snapshots, not SQLite, for the volume.

**What does NOT work here.** Don't replace LightGBM with a deep architecture (DeepLOB, iTransformer, Kronos) until lambdarank+microstructure is wired and validated under CPCV+DSR. The crypto-LOB ablation literature (arXiv 2506.05764) is explicit: "Better Inputs Matter More Than Stacking Another Hidden Layer." Your 422-feature vector with proper objective + microstructure will beat a deep model on raw question text.

### LEVER 3 (high leverage, paper-tradeable, compounds with the combiner): A retrieval-augmented LLM forecaster wired in as the dominant expert in the per-category log-pool

**Why this is high-leverage given your architecture.** Your stat_lgbm sees only the question text. It cannot answer "given today's news, what is the probability?" — that capability lives in your news pipeline, but the news pipeline currently produces a `news_match` scalar (similarity-style) rather than an end-to-end probability. The strongest result in the 2024–2025 forecasting literature is that retrieval-augmented LLMs with reasoning chains produce *probabilities*, not just signals, and those probabilities are competitive with human crowds:

- **Halawi, Zhang, Yueh-Han, Steinhardt (NeurIPS 2024, arXiv 2402.18563)**: GPT-4-Preview + retrieval + self-supervised fine-tune on questions where the LLM beat the crowd → near-crowd Brier on a held-out test set published after model knowledge cutoffs. Open-source code at `dannyallover/llm_forecasting`.
- **ForecastBench (Karger et al., ICLR 2025; Oct-2025 update)**: GPT-4.5 difficulty-adjusted Brier 0.101 vs superforecasters' 0.081, public median 0.107, projected parity in late 2026 (95% CI Dec 2025 – Jan 2028). LLMs now sit *above* the median public forecaster.
- **Turtel et al., "LLMs Can Teach Themselves to Better Predict the Future" (arXiv 2502.05253)**: DPO self-play fine-tuning of **Phi-4 14B** and **DeepSeek-R1 14B** on resolved questions, ranking reasoning traces by Brier-distance to outcome and applying DPO. Result: 7–10% relative Brier improvement, on par with frontier models, *with 4-bit quantization*. This is the most directly relevant result for you because Phi-4-14B and DeepSeek-R1-14B at 4-bit fit in your 16.3 GB of Blackwell VRAM.

**Concrete implementation path.**

1. **Stand up a retrieval-augmented forecaster as a single function `llm_p(question, asof_ts) → p_hat`.** Architecture:
   - Use your existing 30+ news ingest as the retrieval corpus, indexed by your bge-large-en-v1.5 embeddings (you already have this). Restrict to articles with `published_ts ≤ asof_ts` — this is your only protection against retrospective leakage and must be enforced at the SQL layer.
   - Top-k retrieval (k=15–25), then your existing cross-encoder reranker (`ms-marco-MiniLM-L-12-v2`) to top-5–10.
   - Prompt template from Halawi: question, description, resolution criteria, key dates, then top-k summarized articles. Open-ended scratchpad.
   - Elicit *N* independent forecasts (Halawi uses N=6 ensembled across 3 prompts × 2 temperatures); aggregate via geometric mean of odds (logit-space mean) — this is the Halawi recipe and is also what Bayesian Linguistic Forecaster (Sanjay et al. 2025, currently top of ForecastBench) uses as "logit-space shrinkage with a data-dependent prior."

2. **Wire `llm_p` as a fourth expert in your per-category log-pool combiner**, alongside `stat_lgbm`, `market_price`, `news_match`. SLSQP with sum-to-1 generalizes trivially. Empirically, per Halawi and Turtel, the LLM's contribution should dominate in *novel* markets (politics, geopolitics) and be near-zero in *priced-in* markets (sports games already in progress, weather hours from resolution) — the SLSQP weights will discover this if you fit per-category. Critical: log-pool is *external Bayesian* (Genest 1984) and respects relative-propensity consistency, but extreme LLM forecasts (0.1%/99.9%) blow up logarithmically — clip to [0.02, 0.98] before pooling, per the no-regret learning literature (arXiv 2202.11219).

3. **Local fine-tuning on the 5070 Ti.** Phi-4 14B at 4-bit (bitsandbytes nf4) + LoRA r=16 fits in 12–14 GB; Unsloth or Axolotl provide DPO trainers out of the box. Use your 9,933 resolved historical markets:
   - Sample N=3–5 reasoning traces per question with the base model.
   - Score by Brier vs ground truth.
   - Construct DPO pairs: (chosen=lowest-Brier trace, rejected=highest-Brier trace). Discard pairs where Brier difference < 0.05 to avoid noise.
   - Train DPO 1–3 epochs at lr 5e-7 (low — DPO is sensitive). β=0.1.
   This is the Turtel recipe and is a 6–12 hour run on a 5070 Ti.

4. **Free in-flight QA via consistency checks (Karkare et al., arXiv 2412.18544).** For NegRisk events, query the LLM on each outcome separately and check that the implied probabilities sum to ≈1 (your current combinatorial-arb detector does the analogous check on market prices). When the LLM's probabilities violate the sum constraint by > ε, the *consistency score* is itself predictive of Brier — discount the LLM's weight in the combiner accordingly. This is a free signal and pairs naturally with your existing combinatorial machinery.

**Expected magnitude.** On your existing log-pool weights (combiner already has stat_lgbm + market_price + news_match), adding a competent LLM forecaster as a fourth expert should reduce calibrated Brier by 5–15% on news-heavy categories (politics, geopolitics) where the question-only LightGBM has the least information. It should be near-zero or negative on commodity/late-stage sports where market_price already dominates — the per-category SLSQP will route around this. The magnitude is bounded above by the raw LLM Brier (~0.10–0.13 per ForecastBench) and below by the existing combiner; pessimistic estimate is +2–5% relative Brier reduction averaged across categories.

**Risks and tradeoffs.**
- **Retrospective leakage** is the dominant risk and the single critique in the LessWrong "Contra papers claiming superhuman AI forecasting" piece. Your tick-level news capture going forward is leak-free; for backfill you must trust article publication timestamps. Build an "asof-clean" retrieval index and validate it by hand on 50 questions before reporting any Brier numbers.
- **Cost / latency**. Local Phi-4-14B at int4 generates ~30–60 tok/s on a 5070 Ti. With N=6 forecasts × ~600 tokens each per question × 1,150 books, you cannot rerun on every WSS tick. Run it on **event creation** and on **news-velocity triggers** (your news pipeline already computes velocity). Cache the resulting `llm_p` in SQLite per (market, asof_news_hash).
- **The sentiment-ablation lesson does not transfer.** Your equities ablation found FinBERT/FinTwitBERT features were Sharpe-negative. That was for short-horizon equity returns, where news is mostly noise. For prediction markets — especially political and geopolitical markets — news is a *direct* causal driver of outcomes, not a noisy sentiment proxy. The structural relevance is much higher. Keep FinBERT for sentiment z-score, but the LLM forecaster does the heavy lifting via reasoning, not embeddings.

### What probably won't move the needle (be candid)

- **Multi-LLM probability consensus** as a separate combiner expert. Halawi's ensembling already happens *within* the single fine-tuned model via N independent samples — that captures most of the consensus benefit. A second frontier-model API call adds cost and brittleness without much marginal Brier improvement once you have a competent local LLM.
- **Wash-trade filter as a primary signal.** Dubach reports median 1% wash share on Polymarket with a 22% upper tail — well below crypto exchanges. Filter out the 22% tail markets, but don't expect a big Sharpe lift. It's hygiene, not edge.
- **Combinatorial arb expansion beyond NegRisk.** IMDEA: combinatorial arbitrage (cross-event) was 0.24% of total realized arb profits; intra-NegRisk rebalancing was 73%. Your existing detector at 60s already covers the structural part of the latter. Going to subgraph-level cross-event detection is a big lift for a tiny pool.
- **Chronos-Bolt for prediction markets.** Chronos is a time-series foundation model; binary outcomes resolved by an oracle aren't a time-series problem in the relevant sense. Use it only for the rare numerical-bracket NegRisk markets (#views of MrBeast video, BTC at hour H), not the political/event markets that are the bulk of your book.
- **Multi-stage classifier (longshot vs favorite).** The Le 2026 calibration decomposition shows the favorite-longshot pattern is *domain × horizon* — better captured by per-(category × horizon-bucket) isotonic calibration, which is one ENUM column away in your existing pipeline.
- **RL for sizing**, before you have realistic execution. RL trained against optimistic VWAP fills will learn to over-trade and will fail catastrophically on real markets. Wait until Lever 1 is in place, then revisit — and even then, fractional Kelly with a Thompson bandit (your current setup) is competitive with RL on i.i.d. binary bets per the prediction-market Kelly literature (arXiv 2412.14144, arXiv 1201.6655). Half-Kelly captures ~75% of full-Kelly growth at ~50% of the volatility.
- **Wire-to-decision latency profiling**. Useful as hygiene, but the literature (3.6s arb lifetimes, 50ms feed median) tells you that you cannot win the latency game on Windows over public RPC. Measure once, accept the constraint, design for structural maker edge instead.

### Integration order for Polyagent

1. **Week 1–2**: Stand up the queue-aware/pessimistic fill simulator alongside the existing VWAP path; rerun the last 30 days of paper fills under both. This will tell you immediately whether your +$75 is real or a half-spread artifact. Move the trade-hunter to two-sided limit posting in paper. (Lever 1.)
2. **Week 3–4**: Build the snapshot-based training matrix (10–20 snapshots per historical market × microstructure features), retrain LightGBM with `objective='lambdarank'` and `group=event_id`. Run CPCV with market-level purging; report DSR with `n_trials` set honestly. Replace the global isotonic with per-category × horizon isotonic. (Lever 2.)
3. **Week 5–8**: Stand up `llm_p` with retrieval; fine-tune Phi-4 14B via DPO on the 9,933 resolved markets; wire as a fourth expert in the SLSQP combiner with logit-clip and consistency-check weighting. (Lever 3.)
4. **Continuously**: Monitor live-vs-historical calibration drift via PSI on the rolling 200 most recent paper fills. Only consider real-money deployment when (a) pessimistic-simulator P&L is positive over ≥500 fills, (b) DSR > 0 at the achieved n_trials, (c) live-vs-historical PSI is stable.

The compound effect of these three levers — realistic execution + listwise objective with NegRisk grouping + LLM forecaster as combiner expert — is the right "biggest swing" before deploying real money. Each is paper-tradeable, each compounds with what you already have rather than replacing it, and each is grounded in 2024–2026 literature with directly applicable empirical magnitudes.

## Caveats

- **Several headline 2026 numbers are from preprints that have not been peer-reviewed.** The Della Vedova 2.52¢ execution gap, Akey/Grégoire/Harvie/Martineau 76.5%-of-profits-to-top-1%, Yang $121-vs-$63 maker/taker, Dubach 650–900bps half-spread, Le decomposition, and Dubach 59% feed-direction agreement are all from working papers / arXiv preprints dated 2026. The directional finding (execution dominates, makers win) is consistent across all five independent papers, but specific magnitudes may shift on revision. Treat them as best available estimates, not settled truth.
- **The IMDEA "$40M arbitrage realized" headline** (Saguillo et al., AFT 2025) is profit *captured by all arbitrageurs*, not edge available to a new entrant. The 3.6s NBA arb lifetime tells you the marginal new-entrant edge is much smaller than $40M / (number of arbitrageurs) suggests. Plan accordingly.
- **The Halawi 2024 "near human crowd" claim has been disputed** on LessWrong (Mantas Mazeika et al., "Contra papers claiming superhuman AI forecasting") on retrospective-leakage and IR-quality grounds. The 7–10% DPO improvement (Turtel 2025) is more methodologically clean and is what your magnitude estimate should be anchored to, not the Halawi headline.
- **ForecastBench's Brier 0.101 for GPT-4.5** is on a *random* sample of 1,000 forecasting questions across 9 sources — not specifically on Polymarket. Polymarket's question distribution is more crypto/sports/politics-heavy than ForecastBench's; the realized improvement on your distribution is uncertain and could be larger (more direct news inputs) or smaller (more sports markets where the LLM has no edge over the live in-game line).
- **Lambdarank's 3× Sharpe lift in Poh et al. 2021** is on equity momentum — a different domain. The structural fit to NegRisk events is arguably better, but the empirical magnitude is unproven on prediction markets specifically. Plan for "meaningful lift" rather than "3×."
- **Per-category × horizon-bucket calibration requires sufficient resolved-market counts per cell.** With 9,933 historical markets across 7 categories × ~5 horizon buckets, you have ~280 markets/cell on average — adequate for isotonic but on the thin side. Beta calibration (Kull, Silva Filho, Flach 2017) is more data-efficient than isotonic and worth a head-to-head.
- **The DPO recipe (Turtel et al. 2025) discards low-quality reasoning pairs.** With 9,933 resolved markets and N=4 traces each, expect ~5–8K usable preference pairs after filtering — adequate for LoRA fine-tuning, marginal for full fine-tuning. Stick with LoRA.
- **All the maker-side execution wins assume zero or low fees.** Polymarket charges no taker fees on most markets currently, but the negRiskFeeModule allows up to 0.5% conversion fees and the venue is moving toward institutional structure (ICE investment Oct 2025, $2B at $8B pre-money). Build the fee model into the simulator from day one rather than retrofitting.
